OpenTelemetry & the shape of signals

Three signals, one trace_id. A practitioner's guide to logs, traces, and metrics — what each one is for, what each one costs, and how OTel makes them talk to each other.

LOGS+TRACES+METRICS

§ I

The three signals

Each one answers a question
≡ Signal 01
Logs

"What did the code say happened?"

Timestamped, structured event records — the source of truth for what actually happened. Every field you choose to emit is queryable; every record is independently meaningful. When stamped with a trace_id, they slot into the rest of the picture for free.

Shape: the record

⎯ Signal 02
Traces

"Where did this one request actually go?"

A trace is the end-to-end journey of a single request. It's made of spans — units of work with parent/child relationships, durations, and attributes. Stitched together by a shared trace_id propagated across service boundaries.

Shape: a tree

⊟ Signal 03
Metrics

"What's the shape of the system, over time?"

Numeric measurements pre-aggregated over time windows. Counters, gauges, histograms. Cheap to store, ideal for dashboards and alerts — but you lose per-request detail. 1,432 requests collapse into six bucket counts.

Shape: a histogram

Strong at the substance of what happened
Strong at causal flow and latency
Strong at aggregate shape and alerting
§ II

Logs, up close

Structured records · Stamped with trace_id
Anatomy

The record of what happened.

A log record is a structured event: when it happened, how severe, what was going on, and — if it was emitted inside an active span — which trace it belongs to.

The fields that make a log queryable: timestamp, severityNumber (normalized 1–24), body (string or structured), attributes (any key/values you want indexed), and trace_id / span_id for correlation.

Because every field you emit is queryable, logs are the most flexible signal of the three — you can answer questions you didn't know to ask when you instrumented. Look at the rows opposite: same trace_id across all four entries means you can click a slow span and pull the exact log lines emitted while it was running.

Best at
High-fidelity event detail, ad-hoc investigation, audit trails, anything you'll want to filter or aggregate on later. The signal that adapts to the question.

§ III

Traces, up close

A tree of spans · One trace_id
Anatomy

Each request is a tree.

A span is one unit of work — an HTTP handler, a DB query, an LLM call. Spans nest. The root span covers the whole request; children cover the steps within it.

Every span carries a trace_id (same across the whole tree), its own span_id, and a parent_span_id pointing up the tree. That's how the backend reconstructs the diagram you see opposite from a stream of disconnected span records.

Context propagation across services uses the W3C traceparent header — that's the mechanism that lets a span emitted by service B get stitched onto a trace started by service A.

Best at
Debugging latency, understanding cross-service causality, finding which step in a pipeline failed and why.

§ IV

Metrics, up close

Numbers, summarized over time
Anatomy

Detail traded for volume.

A metric is a stream of numeric data points, each with attributes (the dimensions you slice by) and a timestamp. The aggregation happens before storage.

OTel defines a few instrument types: Counter (monotonic — requests, errors, tokens), UpDownCounter (queue depth, active connections), Histogram (distributions — latency, payload size), and Gauge (instantaneous — CPU, memory).

The histogram opposite is what's behind a p99 latency number. Six bucket counts summarize 1,432 requests. You know the shape of the distribution; you don't know which request was slow.

Best at
Dashboards, SLO tracking, alerting. The thing you put thresholds on. Cheap enough to keep forever.

§ V

How they talk to each other

Correlation is the product
01
Logs hold the substance

Logs are the events themselves — what your code saw, what it tried, what failed. Every field you chose to record is now queryable. This is where the answer lives.

02
Traces add structure

Traces nest events into a tree of spans: which step called which, how long each took, what was waiting on what. The shared trace_id is what makes the cross-reference work.

03
Metrics add scale

Metrics step back from individual requests to show aggregate shape — error rates, latency distributions, throughput. The view that drives alerts and dashboards.

Different signals, different strengths. Logs carry the substance — the actual events, with whatever detail you chose to record. Traces add structure: how spans nest, how long each took, which one was waiting on what. Metrics add scale: the aggregate view across millions of events. OTel's contribution isn't any single signal — it's the shared semantic conventions and the shared trace_id that let the three views compose.

An aside · the fourth thing

On baggage

A short detour, because the OTel spec lists a fourth concept alongside the three signals — and you'll hit it the moment you want a user_id from your front door to ride along into your fifth-hop service.

Baggage is a set of key-value pairs that travel with the trace context across service boundaries, propagated automatically via the W3C baggage HTTP header. Same propagation plumbing as traceparent, different cargo.

Where traceparent carries the identity of a request (trace_id, span_id), baggage carries application data: user.id, tenant.id, feature.flag, region — whatever you want every downstream service to see without threading it through every API call.

It's not really a signal in the same sense as logs, metrics, or traces — you don't store and query it. It's a propagation utility that makes the other three more useful by giving them shared context to attach to.

Not automatic on telemetry. Baggage doesn't show up as an attribute on your spans or logs unless you explicitly read it out and attach it. Easy to miss.
It propagates over the network. Anything in baggage rides in plaintext HTTP headers to every downstream service — including third parties. No PII, no secrets.
Keep it small. Every request carries the full set. Add a megabyte to baggage, pay it on every hop.

Envelope

Three levels of nesting

Every signal type wraps its payload in the same three-tier structure:

resourceLogs / resourceSpans / resourceMetrics — what produced this (service, host, pod) scopeLogs / scopeSpans / scopeMetrics — which instrumentation library emitted it logRecords / spans / metrics — the actual data points

The nesting deduplicates resource and scope attributes — you only describe the service once per batch, not on every record.

§ VII

When to reach for which

A practitioner's table
You want to know… Reach for Why
"Is anything broken right now?" Metrics Pre-aggregated, cheap to alert on. The thing your pager talks to.
"Where did the time go for this request?" Traces Per-span durations and parent/child relationships show the critical path.
"What exactly did the code report when it failed?" Logs Stack traces, error messages, structured event data — the long-form detail.
"Is this regression in p99 latency our fault or upstream's?" Metrics → Traces Metric tells you it's regressed; traces show which span got slower.
"How often does this specific code path execute?" Counter If you only need the count, a counter is the cheapest answer. If you'll later want the why, log it.
"Why did this user's request fail at 14:32?" Traces + Logs Find the trace, pull logs by trace_id. The full story.
"Where are tokens being burned?" Span attrs Per-call detail on LLM spans (input/output tokens) beats a coarse counter.
"What's our 30-day cost trend by route?" Metrics For pure aggregate trends, pre-aggregated counters are cheapest at long retention.
§ VIII

Things that trip people up

Confusions, named

Spans, span events, and logs

Three places to put event data, with real overlap. Span attributes describe the whole span; span events are timestamped annotations inside a span; logs are independent records correlated by trace_id. Reasonable shops land in different places — some treat logs as the primary event store and use spans for timing; others lean on span events. The OTel ecosystem supports both; pick what matches how your team actually queries.

Sampling cuts traces, not metrics

Traces get sampled — head-based (decide at trace start) or tail-based (decide after, based on errors / latency). Metrics are unsampled by design; aggregation is the sampling. Logs are usually unsampled but can be filtered.

The collector is not optional in production

Don't send OTLP straight from app to backend. The OpenTelemetry Collector handles batching, retries, redaction, sampling, and fan-out. It's the layer that decouples your instrumentation from your vendor choice.

Cumulative vs. delta temporality matters

A counter with cumulative temporality has been counting since process start. With delta, each export is "since last export." Backends prefer one or the other — Prometheus-style is cumulative; some vendors want delta. Mismatch = wrong dashboards.

Semantic conventions are the whole point

Without shared attribute names — http.request.method, db.system, gen_ai.system — vendors can't write generic queries. Resist the urge to make up your own attribute names where conventions exist.

Cardinality kills metrics

Every unique combination of attribute values is a separate time series. Tagging a metric with user_id creates a series per user — millions of them. Put high-cardinality data on spans or logs where it belongs; keep metric attributes low cardinality.

§ IX

Core principles

Six things to remember
01

Three signals, one trace_id. Correlation is the product. Signals without shared context are three siloed tools in a trench coat.

02

Each signal answers a different question. Logs: what happened? Traces: how did it flow? Metrics: how often, how fast, how many?

03

Pre-aggregate metrics, per-request traces. Cost and detail trade against each other. Pick the right tool for the question.

04

Conventions over creativity. Use OTel's semantic conventions where they exist. Your attribute names are not the differentiator.

05

The collector is infrastructure. Batching, redaction, sampling, fan-out. Don't let apps send OTLP straight to vendors.

06

High cardinality belongs on spans and logs. User IDs, request IDs, prompt hashes — these are queryable attributes, not metric labels.
